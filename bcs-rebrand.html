The problem with having
no system.

When I took over as Head of Marketing at the Business Consulting Society Vienna, the account had 504 posts and no coherent identity. Different fonts, different overlay opacities, different logo placements on every post. It looked like six different people made it — because they had.

Before — Jan 2026
  • No unified template system
  • Inconsistent font usage across posts
  • Random logo placement and sizing
  • Varying overlay opacity on photos
  • No defined content rhythm or strategy
  • Posts felt made in isolation
After — Apr 2026
  • Full template suite across all formats
  • Defined typography hierarchy
  • Consistent logo placement rules
  • Standardized photo treatment
  • Editorial content calendar
  • Recognizable feed at a glance
what
i built ↓
the work

Building a system,
not just templates.

The first thing I did was map what actually existed — every post format, every color usage, every font that had ever appeared on the account. Then I made decisions: what stays, what goes, what gets standardized.

The navy and white system was the right foundation — professional, recognizable, appropriate for a consulting society. What was missing was precision. Exact hex codes, exact overlay opacities, exact logo placement rules. I codified all of it into a template suite that anyone on the team could use and produce something that looked like it belonged.

"The goal wasn't to make it beautiful. It was to make it consistent enough that beautiful could happen reliably."

Beyond templates, I built an editorial strategy — a content mix of workshop recaps, partner announcements, team introductions, and event coverage that gave the feed rhythm and variety without losing coherence. I also expanded from Instagram to LinkedIn, building a dual-platform presence with differentiated content for each audience.

The 7% average engagement rate sits well above the 1–3% industry benchmark for organisational accounts. Peak Instagram reach hit 6,000+ on a single post. The account now looks and feels like a single coherent brand — which is the whole point.

What I'd do differently: introduce an accent color earlier. The all-navy system is consistent but flat — a single warm accent used sparingly would add visual tension without breaking the identity. I'm building this into the formal brand document now before I leave for Ivey.
